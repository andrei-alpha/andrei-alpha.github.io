Cellular Automata and Reversible Computing

 

Replicator

Gemini is a spaceship discovered in 2010 by Andrew J Wade. You might ask, what makes it so special, that it deserves its own page? The same reasons why it has been called by Life enthusiasts “the Life discovery of the millennium”!

First of all, it is the first oblique spaceship – it travels in a direction that is neither orthogonal nor diagonal. While this is a major breakthrough for Life hobbyists, it is the way in which is achieves this that is truly relevant: it moves by self replication.

An immensely long “instruction tape” of gliders carries all the information required to build the spaceship and the tape itself. At both ends of the tape, a spaceship is constructed while its old version is destroyed – thus the name Gemini. The way in which it works is illustrated in this video:

What makes it different from a glider? The crucial aspect is that two partial versions of Gemini coexist, the new one being created from small bits of the old. The entire process takes 33699586 generations.

The creation of Gemini could give us new insight into creating new types of computing machines. Stephen Wolfram seems to think so, he says: “This discovery is helping us understand the world of constructing things from dumb components”. Conceivably, principles like the ones employed in Gemini could be the base of creating molecular-scale computers.
